Correlation Discovered Between Lack of Health Care and Poverty

According to data taken from the American Census Survey for 2014, there is a strong correlation between a state's overall healthcare coverage percentage and the state's overall level of poverty.

An examination of the graphic above reveals that as a state's lack of health insurance increases, they also tend to have higher levels of poverty. Exceptions to this trend include Washington D.C., West Virginia and Kentucky, all with higher levels of health care coverage yet higher than average percentage of their populations in poverty. One similarity between these "states" is their small population size, making it easier for those locations to have more localized version of health care (DC), or when they are employed largely in agricultural and manufacturing jobs (West Virginia and Kentucky), they are unionized with access to healthcare through their employer. More analysis of unemployment rates would be needed to see if employment, and its wages, is not keeping pace with cost increases in these areas. In almost no other states is the extent of the difference between these two factors greater than 10%.

Perhaps the greatest success story of why states should move to a healthcare for all model is Massachusetts. Massachusetts implemented its requirement for all residents to have health care in 2007, along with its MassHealth exchange, and the results appear to speak for themselves: they have some of the lowest poverty rates in the country. More analysis is needed to determine whether there are additional factors such as higher minimum wages or government services that perhaps help workers not fall into poverty.
